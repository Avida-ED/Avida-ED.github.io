This page is an HTML-first migration of the lab book's introductory activity. It is intended to orient students to what Avida-ED is, why digital evolution is scientifically useful, and how to begin using the software.

## Learning goals

Students should be able to:

- explain what Avida-ED is and how it can be used for experiments
- describe how evolution can be studied experimentally
- identify strengths and limits of digital organisms as a model system

## Core idea

Avida-ED is the educational version of Avida, a software platform built to study evolving digital organisms. The instructional goal is not only to teach evolutionary mechanisms, but also to help students practice scientific reasoning with a manipulable model system.

The legacy lab book emphasized a specific claim that remains central to the project:

- evolution requires variation, inheritance, and selection
- when those conditions are present, evolutionary change can occur in digital systems as well as biological ones

## Questions to think about

- What is Avida-ED?
- How does it work as an experimental system?
- Can we study evolution by performing experiments?
- What makes Avida useful for biologists?
- What are the strengths and limitations of this model?

## Suggested activity sequence

1. Read a short background piece on digital evolution, such as the Carl Zimmer "Testing Darwin" article referenced by the legacy lab book.
2. Launch Avida-ED and let students orient themselves to the interface.
3. Use a short video or transcript-guided walkthrough from [Videos & Guides](../videos/index.md).
4. Continue into [Exercise 1: Random Mutation and Genetic Variation](./exercise-1-random-mutation.md).

## Interface orientation

The lab book organized Avida-ED around three main views:

### Population

Use this view to:

- run experiments
- set up environmental conditions
- observe population change
- collect data over time

### Organism

Use this view to:

- examine the genome of an individual Avidian
- watch execution of the instruction sequence
- inspect offspring mutations

### Analysis

Use this view to:

- compare completed runs
- inspect trends over time
- evaluate experimental outcomes with graphs and data displays

## Why start here?

The introduction in the lab book was designed to do two things at once:

- demonstrate the mechanics of the software
- begin building conceptual connections to variation, inheritance, selection, fitness, and scientific modeling

That remains the right approach for the new site. Students should not be dropped directly into controls without a scientific question, and they should not be asked abstract questions about evolution without seeing how the software lets them test ideas.
